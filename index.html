Usage

Gold does not react with oxygen at any temperature.

While silver does not react with oxygen, it reacts with sulfur and sulfuric compounds in the air to form black silver sulfide. This is commonly known as tarnishing.

Copper reacts with oxygen in the air to form a layer of dark brown copper oxide. Unlike iron oxide, or rust, this layer of oxide protects the rest of the copper from reacting with more oxygen.

Aluminium quickly reacts with oxygen to form a surface layer of aluminium oxide. Like copper, this also protects the rest of the metal from further reaction.

Gold is unaffected by most acids. However it does react with selenic acid and can be dissolved in aqua regia. Aqua regia is a 1:3 mix of nitric acid and hydrochloric acid.

While silver is not attacked by most acids, hot sulfuric acid in high concentrate or somewhat concentrated nitric acid will dissolve silver.

Copper tarnishes when exposed to some sulfur compounds, with which it reacts to form various copper sulfides.

Aluminum can react with dilute sulfuric, hydrochloric and nitric acid to make hydrogen gas and aluminium sulfate, chloride or nitrate respectively.

Gold, Silver, and Copper do not react with water.

Gold has a high density of 19.30 g/cm3. This makes it a very heavy metal.

Silver has a medium density of 10.49 g/cm3. This makes it heavy, but only half as much as gold.

Copper is slightly heavy with a density of 8.96 g/cm3 but is on the lighter side compared to most metals.

Aluminium is a very light metal with a density of 2.698 g/cm3.

Gold is the most ductile metal that we know.

Silver is the second most ductile metal that we know of.

Copper has a high ductility compared to most metals.

Aluminium has an average ductility.

Out of all the common metals and alloys, gold is the most mallabel.

Compared to most common metals and alloys, silver is the second most malleable under gold.

Copper is the fourth most malleable metal

Aluminium is the third most malleable metal.

Gold is a shiny metallic yellow. It has been so prominent throughout human history it has defined its own color as gold.

Silver reflects all visible frequencies of light as it absorbs near-ultraviolet light. Like gold this prominent shine has led to silver naming itself.

Copper is a peachy pink color. When oxidised, it becomes a dull brown.

Aluminium is a reflective silvery color but aluminium oxide, which forms very fast on silver, is a whiteish gray. Due to the oxide this is the color we see the most.

Usage

Gold is frequently used in electronics because of a number of its properties. The first is its high electrical conductivity. The conductivity of gold is so high that it is used as a reference point for other metals. A common measure of conductivity is a percentage of how conductive gold is. The second property is its ductility. Gold has the highest ductility of most other metals and this allows it to be drawn out into very small and thin wires. This is essential as gold is very expensive. The third property is its reactivity. Gold does not react with oxygen or water meaning that exposure to earth atmosphere will not degrade the circuit over time.

Although silver is not used in electronics, it matches gold in almost all of its properties. It is more conductive than gold, just as ductile, and just as malleabile. The problem with silver is that it reacts with sulfuric compounds in earth's atmosphere to form a tarnish and degrade. This means that it can't be used in electronics as the tarnish and degradation would render the circuit useless.

Copper is the most common conductor used in electronics. Its high ductility makes it easy to form into wires and its malleability allows it to be flattened into contact pads or electrodes while retaining its shape and not breaking under stress.. Although it forms copper oxide in air, this does not degrade the metal or damage its integrity because the oxide only forms a surface layer. This combined with its low cost and high abundance makes it the best choice for electrical cabling and wires.
